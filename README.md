# Charlie No-Face

> A western-Pennsylvania urban legend about a faceless or 'green-skinned' figure walking rural roads at night. The legend is rooted in the real life of Raymond Theodore Robinson (1910–1985), severely disfigured by a 1919 electrical accident, who walked the country roads of Beaver County after dark for decades.

## Overview

Charlie No-Face — also called the Green Man or the Pittsburgh Green Man — is one of western Pennsylvania's most widely circulated 20th-century urban legends. The legend tells of a faceless, sometimes glowing-green figure who walks the back roads at night, sometimes emerging from a tunnel; teenagers driving through rural Beaver, Butler, and Allegheny counties dared each other to find him. The legend has a documented historical source: Raymond Theodore Robinson, born October 29, 1910, in Beaver County, Pennsylvania. On June 18, 1919, when he was eight years old, Robinson climbed the Morado Bridge near Beaver Falls (which carried trolley wires of 1,200 and 22,000 volts) and was severely electrocuted; he survived but lost his eyes, his nose, and his right arm and was left with extensive facial disfigurement. Throughout adulthood Robinson lived quietly in Koppel, Pennsylvania, supporting himself by making leather wallets and doormats. To avoid the stares of strangers in daytime, he walked along State Route 351 and other quiet roads at night with a walking stick. Drivers and teenagers who encountered him built him into the local 'Green Man' / 'Charlie No-Face' legend, sometimes ascribing his condition to lightning, electrocution, or the supernatural. Robinson, by all family and neighbour accounts, was a friendly man who often stopped to talk with respectful visitors and accept cigarettes or beer in exchange for a photograph. He died on June 11, 1985, at the Beaver County Geriatric Center, almost 66 years to the day after his accident. The Piney Fork Tunnel south of Pittsburgh and the South Park Township 'Green Man' tunnel are folklore extensions; Robinson never lived in or emerged from any tunnel. Wyrdness records the figure as a documented urban legend whose entry should foreground respect for the real person at its centre.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Two parallel nicknames in mid-20th-c. western-Pennsylvania oral tradition: 'Charlie No-Face' (descriptive of the disfigurement) and 'The Green Man' (from accounts that his skin appeared greenish, attributed by legend to electrical injury).
- **Meaning**: 'Charlie No-Face' — descriptive nickname; 'Green Man' — color-claim folk-name.
- **First use**: Both nicknames are attested in regional oral tradition by the mid-20th century.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Documented historical person** _(Beaver County local historians, CBS Pittsburgh, Steel City History)_

The legend is fully explained by the life and nocturnal walks of Raymond Robinson, severely disfigured in a 1919 electrical accident. There is no separate paranormal entity.

### Skeptical

**Folkloric exaggeration of disability** _(Folklorists working on American urban legend)_

The 'Green Man' / 'Charlie No-Face' legend is treated by folklorists as an example of how local communities — particularly teenage car cultures of the mid-20th century — produced and sustained legendary frames around disabled people in their midst.
